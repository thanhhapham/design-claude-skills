# Behavioural Insights for Design Review

*Source: Coglode Cookbook (coglode.com/cookbook)*

These 47 insights cover how humans actually make decisions, remember experiences, and respond to design choices. Apply them when evaluating whether a design is working with or against human psychology.

---

## Attention and Perception

**Picture Superiority Effect**
Images are remembered significantly better than words. Visuals convey emotional messages faster and aid cross-cultural understanding. In design: use images to communicate primary benefits, not as decoration. A listing with strong photos outperforms one with strong copy. Flag designs that lead with text where an image would work better.

**Fluency Shortcut**
Easier-to-understand statements feel more believable and trustworthy. Short, clear copy converts better than long, clever copy. In design: flag dense product descriptions, jargon-heavy CTAs, and multi-clause error messages. The more cognitive effort required to read something, the less the user trusts it.

**Metaphorical Shortcut**
Complex ideas are understood faster through familiar analogies. In design: use comparisons and analogies when introducing unfamiliar features ("like a shopping cart, but for services"). New product categories especially benefit from this — don't assume users will construct understanding from scratch.

**Priming**
Recent exposures shape subsequent decisions. Showing positive imagery or language before a key decision improves conversion. In design: the content immediately before a CTA or checkout step matters. A trust signal, a positive review, or a reassuring image primes the user for a confident decision.

**Contrast Effect**
Distinctive products and elements stand out in memory. Contextual contrast drives recall and preference. In design: primary CTAs must be visually distinct from everything else on the screen (reinforces Von Restorff Effect). In pricing: showing a "standard" option next to a "premium" option makes the premium feel more reasonable by contrast.

**Curiosity Effect**
Humans are driven to close knowledge gaps. Partial information creates intrigue and pull. In design: tease outcomes without revealing everything — "See how your listing performs" rather than immediately showing data. Preview states, blurred content behind sign-up walls, and notification previews all leverage this. Don't overuse — false curiosity (clickbait) erodes trust.

**Rhyme as Reason Effect**
Rhyming statements feel more credible and are better remembered. Short rhymes work as mnemonic anchors. In design: slogans and key feature statements benefit from rhythmic phrasing. More relevant to marketing copy than UI copy, but memorable error-state copy or onboarding hooks can apply this.

## Social and Identity

**Social Proof**
We copy the behaviour of similar others, especially in unfamiliar situations. In design: surface relevant social signals — "12 people viewed this today", "Top-rated seller", "200 sold". The more specific and relevant the social proof, the more it converts. Generic "trusted by thousands" is weaker than "47 buyers in your area viewed this listing". Flag missing social proof in listing cards, seller profiles, and checkout flows.

**Status**
People consume and behave in ways that improve their social perception. In design: badges, tiers, and achievement signals satisfy status needs. Carousell Select, Certified badges, and seller performance tiers all leverage this. Flag designs where a user's status or credibility is invisible — especially for professional sellers who rely on visible reputation.

**Authority**
We comply with and trust those perceived as credible experts. In design: verified badges, professional credentials, platform endorsements, and third-party certifications all signal authority. Flag where authority signals are weak, absent, or buried. For luxury watches, Certified status is the authority signal — its design must communicate institutional credibility, not just a badge.

**Reciprocity**
When someone does something for us, we feel compelled to return the favour. In design: proactively giving value (free price guide, free listing tips, unlocking features) creates obligation to engage or convert. Onboarding flows that give before they ask are more effective than those that immediately demand information.

**Self-Expression**
People use brands and products to communicate their identity to others. In design: personalisation features, custom profiles, and identity signals matter. Allow sellers to express personality in their profile. Allow buyers to express taste through saved collections. The more a product becomes part of a user's identity, the harder it is to leave.

**Storyteller Bias**
Stories persuade more effectively than statistics and create higher perceived value. In design: a listing description written as a story ("I bought this camera for my trip to Japan...") converts better than a spec list. Onboarding flows that use narrative structure are more engaging than step-by-step feature lists. Flag feature announcement copy that uses specs without story.

**Humor Effect**
Relevant humor increases motivation, improves memory, and builds brand attachment. In design: appropriate humor in empty states, success screens, and onboarding moments reduces friction and increases warmth. Must match the product category — humor in a luxury watches flow feels wrong; humor in a Toys & Games onboarding feels right. Carousell brand voice is warm but not comedic by default — apply selectively.

## Decision-Making and Choice

**Prospect Theory / Loss Aversion**
Losses feel approximately 2× more painful than equivalent gains feel good. People are strongly motivated to avoid losses. In design: frame choices in terms of what users will lose by not acting rather than what they gain. "Don't miss this deal" > "See this deal". "Your listing expires in 2 days" > "Extend your listing". Particularly powerful for seller nudges — frame as losing visibility, not gaining it.

**Loss Aversion (applied)**
Specifically: users are more likely to complete a task if they feel they've already invested in it and risk losing that investment. In design: show partial progress early (even artificially), use trial periods that frame inaction as loss, and make sunk investment visible ("You've already added 3 photos — just 1 more for a complete listing").

**Framing**
Identical information presented differently produces different decisions. How something is described is as important as what is described. In design: "90% success rate" frames differently than "1 in 10 fail". "Save S$240/year" frames differently than "S$20/month". For pricing features, frame as savings not as costs. For trust signals, frame as protection not as restrictions.

**Anchoring**
The first piece of numerical information encountered affects all subsequent judgments. In design: show the highest price first in a pricing range. In listings: if showing market price comparison, show the higher "retail" price before the listing price. In subscription tiers: show premium tier first. The anchor sets the reference point for everything that follows.

**Default Effect**
Pre-selected options disproportionately influence decisions — most users accept defaults. In design: defaults are design decisions. What's pre-selected in a filter, pre-checked in a form, or pre-loaded in a setting is the behaviour you're endorsing for the majority. Set defaults aligned with user goals (and business goals where they align). Avoid extreme defaults that trigger reactance.

**Analysis Paralysis**
Each decision depletes our capacity for subsequent decisions. Too many simultaneous options lead to deferral, regret, and abandonment. In design: offer a maximum of 3–5 meaningfully distinct options. Organise choices logically. Eliminate redundant products/options. If more options are necessary, progressive disclosure or a recommended default reduces the decision burden.

**Limited Choice**
A corollary to Analysis Paralysis: 6 options typically outperform 24 in both satisfaction and conversion (Iyengar's jam study). In design: SERP filters with 20+ options reduce engagement. Listing categories with too many sub-categories slow browsing. Curated feeds (fewer, better) outperform exhaustive grids for discovery.

**Fast and Slow Thinking (Kahneman Systems 1 & 2)**
System 1 = fast, automatic, emotional. System 2 = slow, deliberate, rational. Most decisions happen in System 1. In design: high-frequency, low-stakes decisions (browsing, tapping, swiping) happen in System 1 — design for speed and emotion. High-stakes decisions (large purchases, KYC, contracts) benefit from System 2 friction — add confirmation steps, summaries, and cooling-off cues. Impulsive decisions harm long-term goals; add friction for complex, irreversible actions.

**Risk Aversion**
People strongly prefer certain outcomes over uncertain ones of higher expected value. In design: reduce perceived risk at every conversion step. Trials, samples, easy returns, clear money-back signals, and buyer protection labels all reduce risk. For Buy Button: prominently communicating Carousell Buyer Protection directly addresses this. For high-value categories (Autos, Luxury): risk reduction through verification is the primary trust lever.

**Certainty Effect**
We pay a premium for guaranteed outcomes over superior uncertain ones. In design: guarantees, warranties, and protection labels are not just trust signals — they're pricing levers. A "Certified" or "Verified" label allows premium pricing because it removes uncertainty. Design must make the guarantee visible and specific, not vague ("100% protected" is weaker than "Verified by Carousell — returns accepted within 7 days").

**Reactance**
Excessive persuasion or perceived loss of control triggers the opposite behaviour. Forced decisions, hard-sell copy, and manipulative dark patterns cause users to disengage or do the opposite. In design: flag designs that feel pushy (countdown timers on non-scarce items, forced consent screens, excessive pop-ups). Balance persuasion with genuine user autonomy. Provide advance warning of significant changes.

**Sunk Cost Bias**
We struggle to abandon past investments, even when continuing is irrational. In design: users who have invested time (filling a long form, completing onboarding steps, uploading photos) are more likely to complete if reminded of their investment. Progress indicators serve double duty — they show completion distance AND remind users what they've already invested. Also: sellers who've built up reviews and transaction history have high sunk cost — use this to communicate the cost of switching platforms.

**Confirmation Bias**
We seek information that confirms our existing beliefs and discount contradictory evidence. In design: don't fight users' prior beliefs — align with them and reinforce. If a user believes Carousell is for secondhand goods, reinforcing that belief in copy works better than trying to reposition. For persuasion: lead with what users already believe, then extend.

## Motivation and Progress

**Endowed Progress Effect**
Pre-starting progress toward a goal increases the likelihood of completion. A loyalty card with 2 stamps already on it (out of 10) is completed more than one that starts at 0. In design: start profile completion bars at 20–30% rather than 0%. Pre-fill what you can. Frame new users as "already started" rather than "starting from scratch". Seller onboarding that begins with "Your store is 30% set up" outperforms one that begins at 0%.

**Commitment and Consistency**
Once someone makes a public declaration or small commitment, they feel compelled to behave consistently with it. In design: small, low-friction commitments early (tapping "I'm interested in selling", setting a price goal) make subsequent larger actions more likely. Public profile completion creates social commitment. Start with small requests and build gradually.

**Rewards**
Gifts and incentives reinforce behaviour and increase loyalty — but type matters. Intrinsic rewards (recognition, achievement, progress) are more durable than extrinsic ones (cash, discounts) which can undermine motivation once removed. In design: prioritise achievement recognition ("Top Seller", "Fast Responder" badges) over pure discount mechanics. Over-reliance on extrinsic rewards trains users to only act when incentivised.

**Tiny Habits**
Smaller goals increase completion rates and long-term success. Large goals feel daunting; small immediate actions feel achievable. In design: break large tasks into the smallest possible meaningful step. Listing a first item should feel trivially easy. KYC should be chunked into 1-minute steps, not presented as a single overwhelming form. Chain new habits to existing behaviours ("After you respond to a message, bump your listing").

**Feedback Loops**
Real-time information clarifies whether to continue or change behaviour. Closing the feedback loop drives better decisions. In design: show sellers their listing performance in near real-time. Show response rate prominently. Price guidance that updates as sellers adjust their price creates a direct feedback loop. Designs that give no signal after key actions (listing submitted, offer sent) leave users uncertain and anxious.

**Spacing Effect**
Spaced repetition improves retention better than massed exposure. In design: onboarding information delivered over multiple sessions is better retained than a single comprehensive tour. Nudges spread across sessions outperform front-loaded tutorials. Feature discovery works better as contextual hints at the moment of relevance than as an upfront feature list.

**Hedonic Adaptation**
The joy from gains diminishes over time as they become the new normal. In design: features that initially delight users will eventually feel neutral. Maintain engagement through unexpected variety, hidden details, and periodic novelty. For seller tools: regularly surface new insights or capabilities rather than letting the dashboard feel static. For buyers: thematic feeds and curated discovery counteract habituation to the standard grid.

**Goal Priming**
Reminders of a user's own goals increase motivation to pursue them. In design: at decision points, remind users of what they said they wanted. "You wanted to sell your iPhone — it's been 3 days since you listed it. Here's how to get more views." The prime must be near the decision point to work. Used in push notifications and CRM journeys to re-engage sellers and buyers.

## Social Dynamics and Scarcity

**Scarcity**
Limited supply increases perceived value and desire. In design: surface genuine scarcity signals — "Only 1 left", "3 others viewing this now". Must be accurate — false scarcity (fake countdown timers) erodes trust when detected, and Carousell users in SEA are attuned to manipulative patterns from Shopee/Lazada. Genuine scarcity on high-value or rare items (collectibles, limited editions, rare cars) is a legitimate conversion driver.

**Limited Access**
Barriers to entry increase perceived value of what lies behind them. Exclusivity drives desire. In design: Carousell Select and Certified programs create limited access to a quality tier. Seller tiers and verified status create aspiration. In design reviews, flag where valuable features are too freely available (no perceived exclusivity) or too locked behind friction (no pathway to access them).

**Surprise Effect**
Unexpected, personalised gestures create disproportionately positive experiences. In design: unexpected delight moments — a congratulatory message when a first listing goes live, a personalised "Your listing is doing well this week" notification — create positive emotional peaks (links to Peak-End Rule). Surprises must be genuine and sparse; predictable "surprises" lose their effect. Don't announce surprises in advance.

**Collection Bias**
The emotional need to amass related items drives repeat engagement. In design: for categories like Toys & Games (Pokémon cards, figures, sets), design must support the collector mindset — visible set completion, numbered series, community of collectors. Release functionality in waves. Badge systems and achievement collections trigger this directly.

**Nostalgia Effect**
Reminiscing about the past weakens the desire for money and increases willingness to spend. In design: references to shared cultural moments, past versions of beloved items, or "retro" framing can soften price sensitivity. Most relevant for categories with nostalgic product associations (retro games, vintage fashion, classic cars). Not universally applicable — use contextually.

## Experience and Memory

**IKEA Effect**
People assign disproportionately higher value to things they helped create or personalise. In design: involving users in the creation of their listing, profile, or preferences increases their attachment to the output. AI-assisted listing (List with AI) should still give users the feeling of authorship — show suggestions, not final outputs. Let sellers edit and own the final listing.

**Ownership Bias (Endowment Effect)**
Perceived ownership of an item increases its subjective value. In design: trials, "try before you buy", and free-start features create a sense of ownership before commitment. On the seller side: once a listing is live and receiving views, the seller feels ownership over the performance — use this to motivate bumps, repricing, and renewals. For buyers: wishlisting an item creates partial ownership feelings that can motivate purchase.

**Autonomy Bias**
We need a sense of control for satisfaction and engagement. Removing choice reduces motivation. In design: offer choices even when the outcome is similar — "Would you prefer to set your price now or after adding photos?" Both paths lead to the same place, but choice increases engagement. For behaviour-change flows (repricing nudges, KYC prompts): use "4As" — Ask (what do you want?), Advise (here's what we suggest), Assist (here's how), Arrange (we'll handle the rest).

**Storyteller Bias** *(see Social and Identity section)*

## Quick Reference for Design Reviews

When reviewing a design, ask:

| Insight | Question to ask |
|---------|----------------|
| Scarcity | Are genuine scarcity signals visible where they exist? |
| Social Proof | Is relevant social evidence surfaced near conversion points? |
| Loss Aversion | Is the cost of inaction framed as a loss, not just the gain of acting? |
| Anchoring | Is the reference price or highest option shown first? |
| Default Effect | Are defaults aligned with what most users actually want? |
| Endowed Progress | Does onboarding or sign-up feel like it's already started? |
| Framing | Is the same fact presented in the most persuasive true frame? |
| Fluency Shortcut | Is every piece of copy as simple as it can be? |
| Feedback Loops | Does every significant action have an immediate, clear response? |
| Reactance | Does any persuasion element feel pushy or manipulative? |
| Fast/Slow Thinking | Is the right level of friction applied to this decision's stakes? |
| Goal Gradient | Is progress visualised near the end of a task? |
| Peak-End Rule | Is the peak moment and end moment of this flow positive? |
| IKEA Effect | Do users feel authorship over what they create? |
| Certainty Effect | Is the guarantee or protection signal specific, not vague? |
| Fresh Start Effect | Is this a temporal landmark where a re-engagement nudge would land? |
| Present Bias | Is deferred-benefit action (KYC, shipping setup) being made immediately rewarding? |
| Cashless Effect | Is payment friction removed so spending feels less painful? |
| Cognitive Miser | Does every screen minimise the number of effortful decisions? |
| Inaction Inertia Effect | Have lapsed users missed a specific promotion that now needs a fresh frame? |
| Price-Value Bias | Is price framing paired with the right value signal? |
| Centre-Stage Effect | Is the preferred/recommended option in the centre of a choice array? |
| Round Pricing Preference | Are prices rounded where trust matters more than precision? |

## Additional Insights (Coglode Gems)

These complement the Cookbook insights above with more specific behavioural mechanics.

**Fresh Start Effect**
Goals set at temporal landmarks (new week, new month, new year, after a significant life event) achieve higher completion rates. In design: re-engagement nudges land better when timed to natural restart moments ("New month — time to refresh your listings?"). Onboarding after a house move, a new job, or a new year is primed for higher commitment. Use temporal framing in CRM copy.

**Present Bias**
Immediate desires consistently override long-term aspirational preferences. Users know they should complete KYC, set up shipping, or fill their profile — but keep deferring in favour of immediate actions. In design: make deferred-benefit actions feel immediately rewarding. "Complete KYC now and get your first payout in 24 hours" removes the temporal gap. Reduce the perceived time cost of beneficial-but-deferred tasks.

**Hyperbolic Discounting**
People prefer smaller rewards now over larger rewards later — the gap feels larger than the actual time difference. In design: instant gratification beats promised future value. "Get S$5 now" outperforms "Earn S$20 over your next 4 purchases". For seller nudges: immediate visibility improvement is more motivating than promised long-term ARPU growth.

**Cashless Effect**
Invisible payment methods (card, digital wallet, in-app currency) increase spending compared to cash because the pain of paying is lower when money isn't physically handed over. In design: Buy Button and Carousell coins directly benefit from this. Reducing payment friction not only speeds up conversion but increases willingness to pay. PayNow and SPX payment flows that feel frictionless leverage this. Don't make users count or handle money.

**Cognitive Miser**
People avoid effortful thinking and default to mental shortcuts. Any screen that requires significant cognitive effort will see drop-off. In design: every additional option, field, or decision point costs mental energy. Reduce every form to the minimum required fields. Use smart defaults. Auto-fill where possible. Flag any screen where the user is asked to evaluate more than 3–5 options simultaneously.

**Inaction Inertia Effect**
When people miss a promotional offer, they become less likely to purchase at full price — the missed deal creates a reference point that makes the current price feel insufficient. In design: lapsed users who missed a discount window need a fresh framing, not a reminder of what they missed. Don't show "You missed 20% off" — show "Here's what's new". Expired offer states must not be displayed without a replacement hook.

**Price-Value Bias**
How a price is framed and what features are shown alongside it dramatically alter perceived value. Price alone communicates nothing — context creates meaning. In design: always pair a price with a value signal (comparison to new price, market average, or feature list). "S$280" means little; "S$280 — 40% below new price" means a lot. Good Deals badging directly applies this.

**Centre-Stage Effect**
In a horizontal array of products or options, items positioned in the middle receive stronger preference. In design: the recommended or default option in a pricing tier or product comparison should be in the centre position. In a 3-option layout, the middle option is perceived as the balanced, safe choice. Apply to pricing page design, filter chip ordering, and subscription tier displays.

**Round Pricing Preference**
Round numbers (S$50, S$100, S$200) signal trustworthiness and perceived quality better than precise figures (S$48.75, S$97.30). In design: for services, subscriptions, and premium tiers, round pricing performs better on trust. For second-hand goods, precise pricing can signal that a seller has thought carefully about value. Apply contextually: round for trust, precise for deal signal.

**Optimism Bias**
People consistently overestimate positive future outcomes and underestimate negative ones. Sellers often overprice because they're optimistic about demand. In design: seller pricing guidance must be gently corrective without feeling punishing. Frame pricing nudges as helping sellers achieve their actual goal ("Price like the listings that sold in 7 days") rather than correcting an error. Don't trigger defensiveness.

**Post-Purchase Rationalisation**
After completing a purchase, buyers minimise perceived product faults to reduce cognitive dissonance. In design: the post-purchase experience should reinforce the buyer's decision with positive signals ("Great choice — this seller has a 4.9 rating"). Reduce buyer's remorse through immediate confirmation, order tracking, and reassuring communication. A positive post-purchase experience also drives review rates.

**Noble Edge Effect**
Products from socially responsible companies receive higher perceived quality ratings. In design: Carousell's recommerce mission ("giving items a second life") is a genuine trust and quality signal. Surface the sustainability angle where relevant — especially for new user acquisition and in markets where environmental consciousness is rising. Don't bury the recommerce narrative.

**Afterlife Effect**
Consumers engage more with recycling and recommerce when shown what their items become after they're sold or discarded. In design: showing sellers where their items go ("Your camera found a new home in Tampines") closes the emotional loop and reinforces the secondhand economy value. Success states and sold confirmations that acknowledge the item's "afterlife" are more emotionally resonant than generic "Item sold" messages.

**Completeness Effect**
Whole, complete-looking items are perceived as more valuable than visually fragmented ones. In design: listing photos that show the full item (not cropped, not partially obscured) convert better. Incomplete profiles feel less trustworthy. Progress indicators on listing completeness directly apply this — a complete listing feels more valuable because it is more complete.

**Speak-Easy Effect**
Words and names that are easy to pronounce feel more trustworthy and valuable. In design: feature names, button labels, and product names benefit from phonetic ease. "Carousell Select" works. Overly technical or hard-to-pronounce feature names reduce confidence. In copy: prefer familiar, phonetically simple words over sophisticated synonyms. Directly reinforces Fluency Shortcut.

**Choice Closure**
Physical or symbolic completion gestures increase satisfaction with purchase decisions. In design: clear confirmation screens, visible order receipts, and completion animations (the final tap that "closes" a purchase) increase post-purchase satisfaction. The checkout flow should feel like it has a clear end — a moment of completion — not just fade out. This is why success animations and confetti moments work.

**Motivating-Uncertainty Effect**
Uncertain rewards motivate greater effort than guaranteed ones. "You might win" drives more engagement than "You will get 10 coins". In design: variable reward mechanics (spinning wheels, mystery rewards, surprise bonuses) outperform fixed rewards for engagement. Apply carefully — predictable "random" rewards feel manipulative. Best used for gamification and loyalty mechanics, not core transactional flows.

**Transparency Effect**
Nudges remain effective even when people know they're being nudged. Disclosure of persuasion intent does not significantly reduce its effectiveness. In design: being transparent about why certain defaults are set ("We pre-selected the most popular shipping option because 80% of sellers use it") actually increases trust without reducing the default's effectiveness. Honest nudges outperform disguised ones in the long run.

**Trivialization Effect**
Sincere, personal thank-you gestures outperform small monetary rewards for customer appreciation. In design: a personalised "Thank you for your first sale" message is more emotionally effective than a S$0.50 coin reward. For milestone moments (first listing, first sale, 10th transaction), a genuine acknowledgement beats a token incentive.

**Entourage Effect**
Sharing exclusive experiences or benefits with others elevates the sharer's perceived status. In design: referral mechanics work better when the sharer feels they're giving something exclusive ("Give your friend priority access") rather than just earning a discount. Seller tiers and verified status that can be showcased to buyers leverage the entourage dynamic — the seller gains status by association with the Carousell endorsement.

**Lucky Loyalty Effect**
Higher past spending makes random rewards feel more personally deserved. Users who have spent more feel more entitled to loyalty rewards — even random ones. In design: for loyalty and rewards programs, heavy users expect to win more. Design rewards programs that acknowledge tenure and spend history, not just points balance. "As one of our most active sellers, you've unlocked early access" lands better than a generic unlock.

**Temptation Coupling**
Pairing difficult or unpleasant tasks with enjoyable activities increases completion rates. In design: KYC, profile completion, and shipping setup are low-excitement tasks. Bundle them with immediate rewards or enjoyable experiences — complete KYC while your listing goes live, or receive a reward notification immediately after a tedious step. The enjoyable payoff (listing live, first sale) needs to closely follow the unpleasant task.

**Bottom Dollar Effect**
Products receive lower satisfaction ratings when they deplete a user's entire budget. When a purchase exhausts available funds, the pain of paying spikes. In design: for high-value purchases, offer payment flexibility (instalment options, partial payments) to prevent the "bottom dollar" feeling. Avoid designs that prominently show a balance going to zero. For sellers, showing earnings accumulation (not just individual amounts) avoids this effect.

**Category Size Bias**
Items grouped into larger categories are perceived as having stronger influence and broader relevance. In design: Carousell's category architecture affects trust. A "Home & Living" category with 2M listings signals strength differently than splitting it into 10 sub-categories of 200k each. For new or thin categories, consider grouping with related categories to avoid the impression of scarcity. Applies to filter design and category page architecture.

**The 25 Percent Rule**
Social norms shift when approximately 25% of a visible group adopts a new behaviour. Below that threshold, the norm doesn't tip. In design: for new features or behaviours (Buy Button adoption, shipping enablement), showing that 25%+ of sellers already use it creates a tipping-point signal. "1 in 4 sellers in your category now uses Buy Button" is a more powerful nudge than "Try Buy Button".

**Reciprocity Decay**
The obligation to return a favour diminishes rapidly over time. The longer the gap between a gift and the ask, the weaker the reciprocity effect. In design: value-first flows (give free tools, guidance, or insights) need to be followed by the ask quickly — not days later. Onboarding that gives users value and then immediately shows a completion CTA is more effective than delivering value and waiting.

**The Risk of Bundling**
Bundled service packages can reduce individual usage rates because users feel the bundle already provides enough. In design: for subscription or coin bundle products, monitor whether buyers who purchased large bundles actually use more features or less. Bundles can create perceived sufficiency that reduces engagement. Unbundled features with clear per-use value can drive higher engagement.

**Bye-Now Effect**
Phonetically similar words (homophones) can unconsciously prime related behaviour. "Bye" primes purchasing; "Hi" primes greeting. In design: copy near CTAs influences conversion through subliminal associations. CTA microcopy, button labels, and surrounding text affect how users feel about the action. "Get yours now" primes ownership differently than "Learn more first". Subtle but real — optimise CTA copy with this in mind.
